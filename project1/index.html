Summer in New England

About the work

This poster for the New York, New Haven, and Hartford Railroad captures a scene of seaside leisure framed within the bold silhouette of a speeding train. The composition fuses two icons of mid-century modern life: the freedom of coastal recreation and the efficiency of rail travel. Figures lounging on the beach are glimpsed through the train’s outline, suggesting that such idyllic escapes are made possible by the very technology rushing through the center of the composition. It’s an image that sells not just a destination, but a rhythm of modernity—motion and rest intertwined. Yet, beneath its polished optimism, the poster reflects a distinctly industrial view of life, where even moments of relaxation are mediated through the machinery of progress.

Artist:

Maurer, Sascha (1897-1961)

Date issued:

1910-1959 (approximate)

Subjects:

Railroad travel; Railroads; Beaches; New York, New Haven, and Hartford Railroad Company

Chicago

About the work

This vibrant lithograph, produced by Latham Litho & Ptg. Co. for the New York Central Lines, invites travelers to experience the energy and allure of Chicago, a city that epitomized modern urban life in the early twentieth century. With bold colors and dynamic composition, the poster captures the promise of rail travel: the ability to move quickly from one bustling metropolis to another, connecting passengers to commerce, culture, and new experiences. As part of the journey through these posters, Chicago highlights how railroads shaped not just physical mobility but also cultural imagination—painting cities as destinations of possibility while reflecting the era’s social and economic realities.

Date issued:

1910-1959 (approximate)

Subjects:

Cities & towns; New York Central Lines

The design reflects mid-20th century fascination with natural wonders and the allure of travel, inviting viewers to imagine experiencing the power and spectacle of this remarkable landscape.

No rain in Portugal but tourists pour in

About the work

This 1954 Portuguese travel poster combines humor and national pride to advertise a climate of perpetual sunshine. The central figure, a personified sun in traditional Portuguese dress, holds an umbrella while tourists—also depicted in traditional attire from various countries—move energetically across the scene. The composition emphasizes bright colors and playful exaggeration, reflecting mid-century advertising’s optimism and visual wit. At the same time, the caricatured treatment of both locals and foreign visitors hints at the era’s simplified and sometimes stereotypical approach to representing culture.

Publisher:

Lisboa [Lisbon, Portugal]: Bertrand (Irmãos) Lda.

Date issued:

1954

Subjects:

Sun, in traditional Portuguese costume, holding an opened umbrella. Tourists from other countries, also in traditional costume, frolic along the sides.

Tasmania. The anglers' paradise

About the work

This Tasmanian travel poster, printed by Troedel & Cooper for the Tasmanian Tourist Bureau, is striking in its simplicity. Against a bright green background, a single trout is shown beside a tape measure marking 31 centimeters—a modest yet exacting image that functions as both proof and promise. By focusing solely on the fish, the poster appeals directly to anglers, positioning Tasmania as “the anglers’ paradise” through evidence rather than scenery. The design’s precision and lack of landscape suggest an almost scientific framing of nature, one that measures value in size and yield rather than in beauty or experience. In doing so, it reflects a utilitarian strain of mid-century tourism imagery, where travel was often marketed through the quantifiable pleasures of sport and conquest.

Date issued:

1910-1959 (approximate)

Published:

Melb., Vic., Australia : Troedel & Cooper Pty. Ltd., Photo-Print

Subjects:

Tourism; Trout

Alaska via Canadian Pacific: Taku Glacier

About the work

This poster presents the Taku Glacier with a Canadian Pacific ocean liner in the foreground, emphasizing the industrialized accessibility of Alaska through ocean travel. The composition is visually appealing, with bold color contrasts highlighting the icy expanse and the sleek ship, yet it simplifies and sanitizes the rugged Alaskan landscape, giving a romanticized view that prioritizes commercial promotion over geographic or environmental accuracy. The poster reflects the era’s fascination with both the remote and the modern, framing travel as adventurous but comfortably controlled. While the design effectively communicates the allure of a northern voyage, it also glosses over the harsher realities of Alaskan travel and the complexities of the natural environment.

Date Issued:

1910-1959 (approximate)

Subjects:

Glaciers; Ocean travel; Ocean liners; Canadian Pacific Railway Company

Peru of the Incas

About the work

This poster juxtaposes the ancient and the modern, showing local inhabitants at Machu Picchu beneath a massive Pan American Airways plane soaring overhead. The scale of the aircraft against the historic ruins creates an uncanny contrast, highlighting the intrusion of modern air travel into a landscape steeped in history. Printed in 1938, the work frames Peru as both a destination of cultural heritage and a node in emerging global transportation networks, blending admiration for the past with the spectacle of technological progress. However, this framing imposes a Western, commercialized perspective on a deeply cultural site, reducing the lived reality of the local communities to a backdrop for tourism and aviation spectacle.

Date issued:

1938 (approximate)

Subjects:

Mountains; Indigenous peoples; Air travel; Pan American Airways Corporation

Auvergne Chatel-Guyon

About the work

This 1932 poster for Auvergne Chatel-Guyon, created by Robert Falcucci, presents a colorful and slightly abstracted landscape dominated by a large silhouetted tree in the foreground, which frames the scene like a keyhole. Beyond it, a fountain and layered countryside with buildings suggest the region’s spa town character, though much of the detail is intentionally obscured. The composition balances decorative abstraction with a clear focal point, while the bright yellow title at the bottom draws immediate attention. While visually striking, the design prioritizes graphic impact over precise geographic representation, reflecting the period’s modernist approach to promotional imagery.

Around the World through Vintage Travel Posters

from the Collections of the Boston Public Library

Away We Go! is a digital exploration of early twentieth-century travel posters—vivid artifacts from an era often celebrated as the “golden age of travel.” These posters, originally designed to inspire wonder and wanderlust, reveal not only the aesthetic and technological optimism of the 1920s–1950s but also the social and political hierarchies that shaped who could travel, who was represented, and how the world was seen.


Featuring selections from the Boston Public Library’s remarkable collection, this project reexamines the posters as cultural documents that reflect both beauty and bias. Their bold colors and sweeping landscapes invite viewers to imagine faraway destinations, even as they perpetuate idealized and colonial visions of place, modernity, and movement.


Organized as a virtual journey across six continents and nearly 45,000 miles, Away We Go! invites reflection on how travel has been sold, who it has been for, and how its imagery continues to influence our collective imagination. Alongside the archival works, visitors are encouraged to consider what a contemporary “poster for travel” might look like today—one that accounts for global inequality, ecological fragility, and shifting ideas of belonging.
